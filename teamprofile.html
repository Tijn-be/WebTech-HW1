History

The “formula” in Formula 1 refers to the set of rules the sport includes. Every type of car has a different ruleset, and thus every Formula is unique.

The very first car races themselves were held in France all the way back in 1894. These were mostly held on the public roads and were therefore far from safe. After banning races on public driveways, the French decided to organise the very first “Grand Prix” in 1907 with a certain ruleset concerning the size and length of the cars that would attend the race. In the same year, the first permanent racing circuit was opened near to London in the United Kingdom.

After World War II, many airports got abandoned. The unemployed then thought of the idea to use these airports as racing circuits, also competing against each other to build the fastest cars in order to beat boredom. This significantly increased the popularity of racing. However, there was lack of structure, so the Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile, shorthand FIA, adopted the sport, came up with a formal ruleset that truly shaped the sport to what we know today and so, this “Formula 1” was born in 1946.

Formula 1 became an official world championship in 1950’s England and has since grown into the highest-profile form of international single-seater racing. Across its history, the series has combined technical innovation with intense competition between drivers and constructors. Success depends not only on the drivers, but also on engineering, strategy and pit work.

The regulations of Formula 1 were originally created such that there was a great balance between supercharged cars and cars with a normal engine. The first race with these regulations was held in 1946 on September 1st. This race was won by the Italian Achille Varzi in an Alfa Romeo 158 Alfetta. Ever since, the evolving cars used in Formula 1 have come a long way as well.

League Structure

In Formula 1, several teams compete against each other. Starting from 2026, the grid consists of 11 teams. Every team is obliged to select contracted drivers for two cars during each Grand Prix. A season is built around a calendar of Grands Prix and championship points awarded based on race results.

Awarded are individual points as well as team points, which is the accumulation of the individual points of the team’s drivers. In normal races, the first 10 drivers gain points ranging from 25 to 1. For sprint races, the amount of points has a range of 8 to 1 for the first 8 drivers. The driver with the fastest track in the race gains an additional 1 point. Qualification races do not reward points, but the results of these determine the starting lineup of the actual race after.

Races are cut off after 2 hours if completing all laps takes too long. Longer racetracks have fewer laps. During races, drivers must switch their tyres at least once in a pit stop. There are harder and softer tyres, as well as tyres suited for wet racetracks. The Drag Revolution System, abbreviated DRS is allowed to use when a driver is less than a second behind their forerunner and the track follows a straight path. Activating this flaps out the rear wing and reduces air resistance so the driver can possibly overtake their forerunner. For safety reasons, for example after an accident or in heavy rain, a safety car can join the track as the head of all other cars. This ensures the others adjust their speed in order to prevent further consequences.
